# Writing the Rules Before the AI Writes You Off

## Intro

This week I spent an unreasonable amount of time thinking about policy documents. Not the kind of thing that makes for exciting dinner conversation, but here we are. The specific trigger was a question that's been nagging at me for a while: if you're going to let everyone in the company — not just engineers, *everyone* — use generative AI tools, what does a sane set of guardrails actually look like?

I've seen two failure modes play out elsewhere. The first is the company that bans everything and watches employees use personal ChatGPT accounts on their phones anyway. The second is the company that says "use AI responsibly" and then acts surprised when someone pastes the entire customer database into a prompt. Neither is a great outcome. So I ended up spending most of this week in conversation with Claude, Gemini, and GitHub Copilot, trying to draft something that might actually hold up.

---

## Highlight of the Week

Two things stood out.

The first was a framing shift that sounds small but kept changing the shape of every draft: **"conditional permission" instead of "prohibition."** Every time I tried to write a flat ban on something, I'd hit a wall — because a blanket ban on terminal commands, for instance, would prevent legitimate use by engineers, while doing basically nothing to stop a curious non-engineer from running whatever the AI suggested anyway. The more useful frame turned out to be: "you may do this, under these conditions, with this approval, logged this way." Slower to write, but probably the only version that survives contact with actual humans.

The second was the moment I wrote a clause about what I'm calling **"cloud sync folder cascade risk."** The basic scenario: an AI agent running in a locally synced Google Drive folder executes a destructive command — say, `rm -rf` on a directory — and within seconds the deletion propagates to the cloud and every device connected to it. The "cloud is safe" assumption dies quickly once you picture this in motion. Watching non-engineers react to this scenario made it clear that this kind of risk needed to be spelled out explicitly, not buried in a technical appendix.

---

## Insights & Learnings

A few things crystallized this week, none of them particularly comfortable.

The "rules, systems, education" three-layer model sounds obvious in retrospect, but the conversations kept surfacing how easy it is to over-invest in one layer and neglect the others. A well-written policy document means nothing without technical controls; technical controls are circumvented without user understanding; and education without enforcement is just vibes. The boring answer is you need all three, proportionally. I still don't know what "proportionally" means in practice.

The other thing I kept running into: **the policy says "approved services only," but nowhere in any of the drafts do we have actual criteria for what gets approved, a list of candidates, or a process for evaluating new tools.** That's a hole. It's the kind of hole that feels easy to fill in later, which is precisely why it tends to never get filled.

There's also a real tension between writing policy that's legally defensible and writing policy that a non-engineer in sales can actually understand and follow. The four-axis framework (usage category / input category / permission level / logging obligation) is coherent and thorough, and I would give it maybe a 15% chance of being read in full by anyone outside the engineering org. The parallel plain-language summary — "things you can do / things you cannot do / when in doubt, ask" — is probably the document that actually gets used.

## Discussion Summaries

**Log 1 — Full policy structure (Claude)**

This was the most structurally complete of the four conversations. We went through everything: scope (including contractors and temporary staff), explicit coverage of AI agents like Claude Code, Cursor, and GitHub Copilot, a list of prohibited input types, minimum-privilege file operation rules, an 8-step incident response flow, and a tiered disciplinary structure. Two clauses worth noting: Article 5, which positions all AI-generated output as "draft / reference only" rather than anything resembling authoritative output, and Article 22, which mandates an annual policy review. Writing it out like this made the whole thing feel almost reasonable. Then I remembered this is a policy that real people will be asked to follow, and the feeling passed.

*The 8-step incident response flow is very thorough. I look forward to never being able to use it calmly during an actual incident.*

**Log 2 — Strategy meeting minutes (Gemini)**

More of a synthesis log — confirming the decision to add this as a separate annex to the existing employment regulations, identifying cloud sync cascade deletion as the primary risk, and outlining the next steps: legal review with labor attorney and outside counsel, tool selection, budget approval. The framing is solid. The problem is that the schedule is TBD, the specific tools are TBD, and the approval process for tools is also TBD. In other words: good bones, no walls yet.

*"Next actions include budget finalization." A sentence that has ended many initiatives.*

**Log 3 — Drafting the actual clauses (Gemini)**

This session got into the specifics of clause language: approved-services-only, no personal accounts, no autonomous execution mode, no direct operations in cloud-synced folders, no sudo, and — the one I keep coming back to — **no execution of commands you don't understand.** That last one is philosophically interesting to put in a policy document. It's also genuinely hard to enforce. The blacklist of command categories (destructive operations, external execution via curl pipe, privilege escalation, etc.) was moved to a separate annex, which is the right call — it'll need updating more often than the main text, and you don't want to trigger a full employment regulations amendment every time a new dangerous pattern emerges.

*"The employee bears final responsibility and is obligated to fact-check." A sentence that will be cited in exactly one meeting before everyone quietly moves on.*

**Log 4 — Operating framework and plain-language summary (GitHub Copilot)**

This was the most practically useful conversation of the week. Four management axes: usage category, input category, permission level, logging obligation. Terminal commands not universally banned, but only executable if you understand what they do — which is the only honest position to take. The business-facing summary format (can do / cannot do / when in doubt) feels like the deliverable that will actually matter. Also: explicit citation of IPA guidelines, the Personal Information Protection Commission's frameworks, and JILPT — not because regulators will read the policy, but because having the reference chain matters when things go wrong and someone asks why you made the choices you made.

*Citing the IPA guidelines is the policy-writing equivalent of adding "consult a doctor" to a food label. Necessary. Largely ignored. Still correct.*

## What to Try Next Week

The obvious gap is the tool approval process. I want to try drafting the evaluation criteria for what qualifies as an "approved service" — security posture, data handling terms, contract structure, etc. — and see if it can be written in a way that doesn't require a lawyer to interpret every time someone wants to try a new tool.

Also: the blacklist annex needs its own maintenance protocol. A list that nobody is responsible for updating is just a list of the risks that worried someone in 2026. Threat surfaces move. The annex has to move with them, which means someone has to own it. That conversation hasn't happened yet.

And somewhere in there, I should probably figure out what "proportionally" means in the three-layer model. I've been kicking that can down the road for about four days now.
