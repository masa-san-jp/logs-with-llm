# Weekly Update – 2026-04-10

This week has been dominated by policy writing—not exactly thrilling conversation material, but here we are. The specific trigger was a lingering question: if you're going to let everyone in the company use generative AI tools (not just engineers, *everyone*), what does a sane set of guardrails actually look like?

I've seen two failure modes play out elsewhere. First is the company that bans everything and watches employees use personal ChatGPT accounts on their phones anyway. Second is the company that says "use AI responsibly" and then acts surprised when someone pastes the entire customer database into a prompt. Neither works well. So I spent most of this week in conversation with Claude, Gemini, and GitHub Copilot, trying to draft something that might actually hold up.

## Highlights

- **Conditional permission over prohibition**: Every time I tried to write a flat ban, I hit a wall. Blanket prohibitions on terminal commands would stop legitimate engineering use while doing nothing to prevent curious non-engineers from running AI suggestions anyway. The better frame turned out to be "you may do this, under these conditions, with this approval, logged this way."

- **Cloud sync folder cascade risk**: I wrote a clause about what I'm calling "cloud sync folder cascade risk"—when an AI agent running in a locally synced Google Drive folder executes a destructive command and within seconds the deletion propagates to the cloud and every connected device. The "cloud is safe" assumption dies quickly once you picture this scenario.

- **The policy-practice gap**: We have "approved services only" in every draft, but nowhere do we have actual criteria for what gets approved, a list of candidates, or a process for evaluating new tools. This is the kind of hole that feels easy to fill later, which is precisely why it tends to never get filled.

## What I Worked On

The week split into four major conversations, each with a different AI system, each approaching the problem from a different angle.

**Log 1** was the most structurally complete conversation with Claude. We went through everything: scope (including contractors and temporary staff), explicit coverage of AI agents like Claude Code and GitHub Copilot, prohibited input types, minimum-privilege file operation rules, an 8-step incident response flow, and a tiered disciplinary structure. Two clauses worth noting: Article 5 positions all AI-generated output as "draft/reference only" rather than authoritative, and Article 22 mandates annual policy review. Writing it out like this made the whole thing feel almost reasonable—until I remembered real people would be asked to follow it.

**Log 2** was more synthesis than drafting—confirming the decision to add this as a separate annex to existing employment regulations, identifying cloud sync cascade deletion as the primary risk, and outlining next steps: legal review with labor attorney and outside counsel, tool selection, budget approval. The framing is solid, but the schedule is TBD, the specific tools are TBD, and the approval process is also TBD.

**Log 3** got into the specifics of clause language: approved-services-only, no personal accounts, no autonomous execution mode, no direct operations in cloud-synced folders, no sudo, and—the one I keep coming back to—**no execution of commands you don't understand**. That last one is philosophically interesting to put in a policy document and genuinely hard to enforce. The blacklist of command categories got moved to a separate annex, which is the right call—it'll need updating more often than the main text.

**Log 4** was the most practically useful conversation. We developed a four-axis framework: usage category, input category, permission level, logging obligation. Terminal commands aren't universally banned, but only executable if you understand what they do—the only honest position to take. The business-facing summary format (can do / cannot do / when in doubt) feels like the deliverable that will actually matter.

## Decisions & Tradeoffs

The "rules, systems, education" three-layer model sounds obvious in retrospect, but the conversations kept surfacing how easy it is to over-invest in one layer while neglecting the others. Well-written policy means nothing without technical controls; technical controls get circumvented without user understanding; education without enforcement is just vibes. The boring answer is you need all three, proportionally.

There's also real tension between writing policy that's legally defensible and writing policy that someone in sales can actually understand and follow. The four-axis framework is coherent and thorough—I'd give it maybe a 15% chance of being read in full by anyone outside engineering. The parallel plain-language summary is probably what actually gets used.

The conditional permission approach required more words than simple prohibitions, but it's the only version that survives contact with actual business needs. "No terminal commands" breaks engineering workflows. "Terminal commands only if you understand them, only on development systems, only with approval" is clunky but workable.

## Progress Since Last Time

This is my first post in this format, so there's no direct comparison point. But compared to where we were a month ago—scattered conversations about AI governance with no concrete framework—we now have draft policy language, a decision matrix for tool evaluation, and a realistic implementation timeline.

The cloud sync cascade risk wasn't on anyone's radar before this week. Now it's prominently featured in every draft, with specific technical controls. That feels like genuine progress on a problem that could have blindsided us.

## What's Next

The obvious gap is the tool approval process. I want to try drafting evaluation criteria for what qualifies as an "approved service"—security posture, data handling terms, contract structure, etc.—and see if it can be written so you don't need a lawyer to interpret it every time someone wants to try a new tool.

The blacklist annex needs its own maintenance protocol. A list nobody's responsible for updating is just a record of what worried someone in 2026. Threat surfaces move; the annex has to move with them.

And somewhere in there, I should probably figure out what "proportionally" means in the three-layer model. I've been kicking that can down the road for about four days now.
