# Events vs. State in Practice

My latest database design experiment forced a fundamental question: should I store what happened, or what currently is? This deceptively simple choice ripples through every layer of system architecture.

## Facts: The Immutable Foundation

I think the most profound insight this week came from revisiting Rich Hickey's distinction between facts and state. When designing membership systems, the conventional approach stores status columns—active, suspended, withdrawn—that get updated as circumstances change.

But here's the mechanism I've been experimenting with: treat every status change as an immutable fact. Instead of `UPDATE members SET status = 'withdrawn'`, I log `INSERT INTO membership_events (event_type, occurred_at, member_id)`. The tactility of this approach becomes clear when debugging—rather than asking "why is this member withdrawn?" I can trace the exact sequence of events that led to that state.

That said, this creates an interesting interplay between storage and computation. Current state becomes derived data, calculated from the event stream using window functions or materialized views.

## Design Systems: The Reproducible Apparatus

Working with Claude Design this week reinforced how critical upfront investment becomes for any creative system. The tool requires 1-2 hours of initial setup, uploading screenshots and establishing design patterns that automatically apply to future projects.

One might wonder whether this overhead justifies the benefits. But I suspect the real value lies in forcing explicit decisions about visual language. The mechanism mirrors my database approach—instead of making ad-hoc styling choices (the equivalent of status updates), you establish facts about your visual identity that generate consistent outputs.

The granularity of this setup proves crucial. Rather than dumping entire codebases, the system works best with specific directories like `tailwind.config.js` or `/src/components/`. In other words, curated facts produce better emergent patterns than raw data dumps.

## Training: Organizational vs. Individual Change

Perhaps the most complex system I tackled was designing AI training for disability welfare organizations. The hypothesis underlying the entire curriculum is that sustainable adoption requires shifting from individual experimentation to organizational standardization.

The pervasive pattern I see across failed technology adoptions is keeping useful discoveries isolated. When someone develops an effective prompt for incident analysis, that insight often dies with their departure or gets buried in personal folders.

Conversely, the training emphasizes establishing AI coordinators, shared prompt libraries, and monthly review cycles. The friction here isn't technical—it's cultural. Organizations without improvement cultures struggle to institutionalize any innovation, regardless of how powerful the underlying technology.

## Resolution: What to Store, What to Derive

These three projects—database design, design systems, and training curriculum—all grapple with the same fundamental question: what should be preserved as fact versus what should be computed on demand?

I believe the pattern emerging from my practice suggests storing intentions and events while deriving current state. Database events capture what actually happened. Design systems preserve visual decisions. Training materials document organizational commitments to specific practices.

The ubiquity of this pattern across such different domains makes me wonder whether it represents something deeper about sustainable system design. When we can trace how we arrived at any current condition, we maintain the capacity for deliberate change.
