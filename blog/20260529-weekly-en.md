# Boundaries That Teach

Claude Design felt less like a single tool and more like a small apparatus with a very specific mechanism. Once I looked at the access path, the two-pane interface, and the first-time design-system setup, I started to see a pattern: the real work is not only making things, but deciding what should be reproducible, what should stay local, and where friction is worth keeping.

## Access as mechanism: what the tool actually permits

The first thing I noticed was that Claude Design is not just “available” in a generic sense. It requires Claude Pro or above, works only in the browser, and is disabled by default in Enterprise unless an administrator turns it on; in other words, access itself is part of the design.

I think that matters because the mechanism shapes the workflow before any prompt is written. The left pane for chat and the right pane for canvas make the interaction feel split between explanation and artifact, and that split has a certain tactility to it: I am not merely asking for output, I am negotiating with a visible surface.

## Design-system setup: one-time work, lasting texture

The setup step asks for a surprising amount of care at the beginning. If I already have a product, the recommended path is to gather 5–10 screenshots, especially top pages, dashboards, and form-heavy screens, plus error states or mobile views if possible.

That said, the point is not just collection. The mechanism seems to be that Claude extracts color, typography, components, and layout patterns from these assets, then applies them across later projects; so the initial investment has high granularity and then becomes pervasive. I suspect that is why skipping setup leads to generic results: without a shared hypothesis about the brand, the system has no stable basis for emergence.

## Inputs that make the system reproducible

I was struck by how specific the guidance is about what to feed in. If the codebase is a monorepo, the recommendation is to avoid handing over the whole thing and instead pass only the relevant subdirectories, such as `tailwind.config.js`, `/src/components/`, or `/src/styles/variables.css`.

On the other hand, if there is no existing brand, Claude can still work from two HEX colors, a font name, and optionally a logo. One might wonder whether this is just a convenience feature, but I think it is closer to a reproducible apparatus for bootstrapping identity: competitor colors from DevTools, a font from Google Fonts, and a few reference documents can become enough texture for the system to infer a coherent direction.

## Prompting and review: structure before polish

The logs also made the prompt shape feel unusually concrete. A 150–300 word prompt that includes purpose, audience, required elements, and constraints seems to be the sweet spot, and the examples point to a practical rhythm: generate, inspect, revise, then hand off.

I believe the most useful distinction here is between structural changes and local changes. Chat is better for broad moves like darkening the palette or moving metrics upward, while inline comments are for local friction such as padding or dropdown behavior; if so, the workflow is less “write once” and more “decompose, adjust, reconnect.”

## Export and handoff: where the boundary lives

The export options make the boundary very explicit. `.zip` for backup, PDF for stakeholders, PPTX for presentation, standalone HTML for publishing, and `Handoff to Claude Code` for implementation all suggest that Claude Design is meant to stop at the exploration phase rather than absorb the whole build.

That seems wise to me. The moment of export is where the interplay between design and code becomes visible: design preserves enough resolution to communicate intent, while Claude Code takes over the mechanism of implementation. I suspect this boundary is what keeps the system useful instead of opaque.

## What I am taking into my own practice: constraints as guidance

This week’s logs also reminded me of another pattern I keep returning to: systems become easier to work with when their boundaries are not hidden. The disability-welfare training design, the event-based DB model, and even the Agent-Aiko versus nullevi03 comparison all point to the same insight in different forms: keep the mechanism inspectable, keep the state visible, and keep the rules reproducible.

In my own practice, I want to keep asking what should be stored as fact, what should be derived as state, and what should be handed off at the right boundary. That feels like a small discipline, but perhaps it is also the only way to preserve tactility while still building at scale.
